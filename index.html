Analysis of global trends in coffee trade¶

Introduction¶

Coffee is a major part of many people's daily life. In fact, it is the third most popular drink in the world, beside water and tea, with over one billion people drinking coffee daily. Despite it's popularity, coffee can only be grown in within a region called the 'Coffee Belt' — a region roughly between 25° North and 30° South of the equator. As a result, the coffee market relies on healthy international trade. With such high demand, signficant strain is put on the countries and workers who produce coffee, raising concerns about the ethicality of the coffee market. The goal of this project is to better understand patterns in the global trade of coffee. I will be exploring a Kaggle dataset that contains data on several facets of coffee trade, including exportation, importation, and production of coffee. I would like to answer the questions of:

-Which countries are the largest exporters, importers, and consumers of coffee?

-Do imbalances within the global coffee trade exist?

-How has the coffee market changed over time?

Conclusion¶

A common theme in the data is that the majority of the world relies on the production and exportion of a select number of countries that have favorable conditions for growing coffee. In addition, Brazil is the largest exporter, which naturally raises questions about the ethical implications of such high coffee exports. While conditions vary greatly from farm to farm, some farms in Brazil use slaves — some as young as thirteen to harvest coffee. (news.trust.org) This problem isn't limited to Brazil either. Coffee must often be picked by hand, which requires long hours of manual labor in potentially dangerous conditions where workers face risks such as snake bites. As our climate warms, it will be even more difficult to grow coffee, and more pressure will be put on workers. However, a recent shift in the coffee market toward high quality, single origin coffee farms, often called 'third wave coffee' is contributing to an ethical and thriving coffee market. By being more concious of where our dollar goes, we can help support the local economies of the countries which produces a drink that is so loved by many. I was surprised to learn that countries like Brazil have such a dominant position in the coffee market, and how much more coffee is exported than imported. A major limitation of my analysis is that this data does not include the last five years, in which time major changes, such as tariffs, have made major changes in the market. In observing my interpretation of the data, someone might be less likely to buy coffee from a large exporter, such as Brazil or Columbia, without scrutinizing the ethicality of another country, as I only graphed the top countries for readability. I believe that I was able to mostly answer my questions, however, more analysis of the individual working and other economic conditions are needed to better understand the market as a whole.
