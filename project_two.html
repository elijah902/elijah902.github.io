Introduction

Recommendation systems have becom the primary method for people to discovery new music. While these systems offer unpararlleled convenience, they don't always accurately capture individual preferences. Platforms like Spotify have recently integreated AI features that allow users to recive curated playlists from input prompts. As these algorithms grow more sophisticated, important questions about their impact on listeners emerge.

While algorithms effectively recommend similar music, they don't always feel right intuitively with listeners a phenomenon known as the 'vibe gap'.

This project aims to explore the metadata featuers that a company might use to power their recommendation systems. More importantly, I want to investigate how recommendation systems might navigate the "uncanny valley" of music curationthe uncomfortable space where suggestions feel almost right but miss the mark. My central question is: How can these systems assist users in discovering music without overly controlling or constraining their tastes?

By using a Random Forest classification model to predict target genres, I will seek to identify pathways toward intelligent recommendation systems that serve to enhance rather than dicate musical discovery.

I will be using a Random Forest classifier because it is flexible and a good indicator of feature importance. It works by generating multiple randomly created decision trees, then combines their outputs into a majority vote to make a final decision. While Random Forest models are robust and are resitant to overfitting, they come with a trade off: the tree making and predicting process is computationally intensive and time consuming.

Discussion

While my model did not perform as accurately as ideally desired, I learned several important insights in this project. Firstly, it is easier for a classification model to identify common patterns than it is to classify outliers. In the context of music recommendation systems, this can manifest in the aforementioned "vibe gap", in which a recommendation system tends to recommended similar music in terms of sonic qualities, rather than what is likely to feel right to a listener.

A major limitation of my dataset was that the data was highly imbalanced, and some genres were represented by very few samples. While a streaming platform has the means to collect larger and more varied data, music is naturally unbalanced, and it may be more difficult to accurately train a model to predict smaller genres. This may lead to users who listen to popular music finding apt recommendations while users who have more niche tastes feeling underserved.

Feature importance analysis revealed that attributes such as length, bpm, and danceability were most important in predictions. This highlights how recommendation systems may prioritize certain sonic features over others, potentially shaping listening experiences.

Despite these challenges, I was able to find out some answers to my questions, such as the factors that go into recommendation. I feel that more exploration is needed, especially with a larger dataset, to understand how a recommendation system could better cater to a variety of musical tastes.

Music recommendation has a significant social impact. Music is an important part of people's emotional lives, and recommendation systems play a direct role in how a person listens to music. In this project, I group many different kinds of genres, which came from only the most popular songs on Spotify. This is a very generalized and narrow view of the music world, and someone reading this project may feel that their preferred genres are seemingly less important than others in music recommendation systems. Positively, I feel that this project can help people understand the algorithms that they interact with daily, and raise awareness of how imporvements in these systems can help serve diverse emotional and musical tastes.
