Introduction

In this project, I will be analyzing coffee metrics using K-means clustering to uncover patterns in the data. In case you're unsure, a coffee variety is like a specific type similar to how Granny Smith is a kind of apple, or a Golden Retriever is a kind of dog. My primary goal in this project is to uncover how the variety of coffee changes the tasting qualities. Beyond flavor, the variety also influences factors import to growers, such as disease resistance, yield, difficulty of growing, etc. It's clear to many that different regions of the world have unique flavors, such as fruity flavors from Ethiopia or chocolately notes in Brazilian coffee. Less people including myself however, have little understanding of the varietal's role in the final cup. I'll explore the data to gain a fuller understanding of this unique aspect of coffee.

I found this data on DataCrayon.com in an article exploring the connections between variety and country of origin. The author of the article forked the dataset from Github, which was originally web scraped from the Coffee Quality Institute website.

Visualization

The different qualities of each coffee, like aroma and sweetness, are measured on a scale from 1-10. According to the Specialty Coffee Association, these scores are based on strength of the metric, rather than enjoyability. Let's first plot the different metrics to see if we can find any preliminary patterns.

In[45]:
import plotly.express as px

                'Balance', 'Sweetness']

fig = px.scatter_matrix(
                    df,
                    dimensions=quality_cols,

The tasting metrics all seem to be highly correlated with each other, and center around similar values, except for sweetness. This could be due to taster culture, with them being unlikely to rate coffees as very weak or strong, or some other factor.
